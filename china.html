The Indus Valley Civilization, also known as the Harappan Civilization, was one of the world's earliest urban societies. It flourished in the Indus River basin, covering parts of modern-day Pakistan and northwest India. The civilization is renowned for its advanced city planning, including the use of standardized bricks and a sophisticated drainage system.

City Description
Mohenjo-Daro One of the largest cities of the Indus Valley Civilization, known for its well-planned layout, including a complex drainage system, large public baths, and standardized brick buildings.
Harappa Another key city, notable for its advanced urban planning and extensive trade networks. It features a large citadel and residential areas separated by streets.
Dholavira Renowned for its sophisticated water management system, including reservoirs, canals, and an advanced water supply network. Dholavira also features a unique layout with a fortified central area.
Lothal Significant for its dockyard, which indicates a thriving maritime trade. Lothal's port was one of the most advanced of its time, facilitating trade with the Persian Gulf region.

Timeline

  • 3300 BCE: Early Harappan Phase begins with small, dispersed settlements.
  • 2600 BCE: Mature Harappan Phase starts, with the emergence of large urban centers.
  • 1900 BCE: Late Harappan Phase, with noticeable decline in urban planning and trade.
  • 1300 BCE: The civilization gradually fades, possibly due to environmental or social factors.
  • 1920s CE: Rediscovery of the Indus Valley Civilization through archaeological excavations.
